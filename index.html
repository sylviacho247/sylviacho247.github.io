About Me

I am a PhD Candidate and Sessional Instructor in the Department of Linguistics at Simon Fraser University, located on the unceded Traditional Coast Salish Lands including the Tsleil-Waututh (səl̓ilw̓ətaʔɬ), Kwikwetlem (kʷikʷəƛ̓əm), Squamish (Sḵwx̱wú7mesh Úxwumixw) and Musqueam (xʷməθkʷəy̓əm) Nations.

My research examines how multilingualism shapes speech across linguistic, social, and acoustic dimensions. Specifically, I am interested in how bilingual and heritage speakers—such as Korean-Canadians in Vancouver—adjust their voice and articulation across languages, and how these shifts are perceived. Using experimental methods, I explore features like voice quality, vowel space, and prosody in relation to language experience. I am also interested in how multilingual speakers contribute to the evolving soundscape of Canadian English, participating in or resisting ongoing sound changes.

In addition to my research, I teach courses in speech science and introductory linguistics, aiming to make phonetics accessible and relevant to students from all backgrounds.

When I’m not working, you’ll probably find me cooking, cleaning, or out on a long walk, reflecting on all the things I’m grateful for.
